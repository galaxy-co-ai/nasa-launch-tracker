Orion

The **Orion Multi-Purpose Crew Vehicle** ( **MPCV**) is a [partially reusable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reusable_spacecraft "Reusable spacecraft") crewed spacecraft used in [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA")'s [Artemis program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_program "Artemis program"). The spacecraft consists of a Crew Module (CM) [space capsule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_capsule "Space capsule") designed by [Lockheed Martin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lockheed_Martin "Lockheed Martin") that is paired with a [European Service Module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Service_Module "European Service Module") (ESM) manufactured by [Airbus Defence and Space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airbus_Defence_and_Space "Airbus Defence and Space"). Capable of supporting a crew of four beyond [low Earth orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit "Low Earth orbit"), Orion can last up to 21 days undocked and up to six months docked. It is equipped with [solar panels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_panels_on_spacecraft "Solar panels on spacecraft"), an [automated docking system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA_Docking_System "NASA Docking System"), and [glass cockpit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glass_cockpit "Glass cockpit") interfaces. Orion is launched atop a [Space Launch System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Launch_System "Space Launch System") (SLS) rocket, with a tower [launch escape system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Launch_escape_system "Launch escape system").

Orion was conceived in the early 2000s by Lockheed Martin as a proposal for the [Crew Exploration Vehicle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crew_Exploration_Vehicle "Crew Exploration Vehicle") (CEV) to be used in NASA's [Constellation program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constellation_program "Constellation program") and was selected by NASA in 2006. After the cancellation of the Constellation program in 2010, Orion was extensively redesigned for use in NASA's Journey to Mars initiative; later named Moon to Mars. The SLS became Orion's primary launch vehicle, and the service module was replaced with a design based on the [European Space Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Agency "European Space Agency")'s [Automated Transfer Vehicle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automated_Transfer_Vehicle "Automated Transfer Vehicle"). A development version of Orion's crew module was launched in 2014 during [Exploration Flight Test-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exploration_Flight_Test-1 "Exploration Flight Test-1"), while at least four test articles were produced. Orion was primarily designed by [Lockheed Martin Space Systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_Space_Systems "Lockheed Martin Space Systems") in [Littleton, Colorado](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Littleton,_Colorado "Littleton, Colorado"), with former [Space Shuttle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Shuttle "Space Shuttle") engineer Julie Kramer White at NASA as Orion's chief engineer.[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-6)

As of 2026[\[update\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Orion_(spacecraft)&action=edit), three flight-worthy Orion spacecraft were under construction, with two completed, one of those two flown and an additional one ordered,[\[a\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-9) for use in NASA's Artemis program. The first completed unit, CM-002, was launched on November 16, 2022, on [Artemis I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_I "Artemis I").[\[9\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-Kraft_May162022-10)[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-CNN_Storm_Nicole_Report-11)[\[11\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-nasa-20221108-12)

In May 2025, the [second Trump administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_presidency_of_Donald_Trump "Second presidency of Donald Trump") proposed terminating the Orion spacecraft program after Artemis III.[\[12\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-:3-13) The [One Big Beautiful Bill Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_Big_Beautiful_Bill_Act "One Big Beautiful Bill Act") signed in July 2025 included a provision to fund procurement of Orion for Artemis IV and reuse in future missions.[\[13\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-14)

## Description

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Cev_design.jpg/500px-Cev_design.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cev_design.jpg) Configuration of the Orion spacecraft. The capsule shown in the photo is an early design version of Orion.[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8a/Orioncm.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Orioncm.jpg) Crew module[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Spaceships.svg/500px-Spaceships.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Spaceships.svg) Currently operational crewed spacecraft (at least orbital class)[![Interactive 3D models of the spacecraft, with the spacecraft on the right in exploded view.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Orion_Spacecraft.stl/500px-Orion_Spacecraft.stl.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Orion_Spacecraft.stl) Interactive 3D models of Orion, with the spacecraft fully integrated on the left and in exploded view on the right

Orion uses the same basic configuration as the [Apollo command and service module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_command_and_service_module "Apollo command and service module") (CSM) that first took astronauts to the Moon, but with an increased diameter, updated thermal protection system, and other more modern technologies. It is designed to support long-duration deep space missions with up to 21 days of active crew time plus 6 months' quiescent spacecraft life.[\[14\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-C4WDefault-7291456-15) During the quiescent period, crew life support would be provided by another module, such as the proposed [Lunar Gateway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_Gateway "Lunar Gateway"). The spacecraft's life support, propulsion, thermal protection, and avionics systems can be upgraded as new technologies become available.[\[15\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-Space.com1-16)

At launch, the Orion spacecraft includes both crew and service modules, a spacecraft adapter and an emergency launch abort system. The Orion's crew module is larger than [Apollo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_program "Apollo program")'s and can support more crew members for short or long-duration missions. The [European service module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_service_module "Orion service module") propels and powers the spacecraft as well as storing oxygen and water for astronauts. Orion relies on solar energy rather than fuel cells, which allows for longer missions.

### Crew module (CM)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/The_Artemis_II_crew_completing_Post_Insertion_and_Deorbit_Preparation_training_%28jsc2025e004086%29.jpg/500px-The_Artemis_II_crew_completing_Post_Insertion_and_Deorbit_Preparation_training_%28jsc2025e004086%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:The_Artemis_II_crew_completing_Post_Insertion_and_Deorbit_Preparation_training_(jsc2025e004086).jpg) The crew of Artemis II training inside an Orion mock-up in January2025[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Orion_Spacecraft_Outfitted_Interior_2021_%28labeled%29.jpg/500px-Orion_Spacecraft_Outfitted_Interior_2021_%28labeled%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Orion_Spacecraft_Outfitted_Interior_2021_(labeled).jpg) Annotated photo of the Orion mock-up, outfitted to fully simulate a crewed mission, 2021 [![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/NASA%27s_Orion_Spacecraft_Parachutes_Tested_at_U.S._Army_Yuma_Proving_Ground.jpg/500px-NASA%27s_Orion_Spacecraft_Parachutes_Tested_at_U.S._Army_Yuma_Proving_Ground.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:NASA's_Orion_Spacecraft_Parachutes_Tested_at_U.S._Army_Yuma_Proving_Ground.jpg) Testing of Orion's parachute system[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Orion_PA-1_paint_job.jpg/500px-Orion_PA-1_paint_job.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Orion_PA-1_paint_job.jpg) Orion Crew Module Model ( [Neil A. Armstrong Flight Research Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neil_A._Armstrong_Flight_Research_Center "Neil A. Armstrong Flight Research Center"))

The Orion crew module (CM) is a reusable transportation capsule that provides a [habitat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat "Habitat") for the crew, provides storage for consumables and research instruments, and contains the docking port for crew transfers.[\[15\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-Space.com1-16)[\[16\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-17)[\[17\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-NSF-20141030-18) The crew module is the only part of the spacecraft that returns to Earth after each mission and is a 57.5° [frustum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frustum "Frustum") shape with a blunt spherical aft end, 5.02 meters (16ft 6in) in diameter and 3.3 meters (10ft 10in) in length,[\[18\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-19) with a mass of about 8.5 metric tons (19,000lb). It is manufactured by the [Lockheed Martin Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_Corporation "Lockheed Martin Corporation") at [Michoud Assembly Facility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michoud_Assembly_Facility "Michoud Assembly Facility") in [New Orleans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Orleans "New Orleans"), Louisiana.[\[19\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-20)[\[20\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-LaNasaMichoud2021-21)[\[21\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-WGNO2021-22)[\[22\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-23) It has 50% more volume than the Apollo capsule and will carry four astronauts.[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-ref_manual-1) After extensive study, NASA selected the [Avcoat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AVCOAT "AVCOAT") [ablator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ablation "Ablation") system to provide heat protection encountered during reentry for the Orion crew module. Avcoat, which is composed of [silica fibers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silica_fiber "Silica fiber") with a resin in a honeycomb made of [fiberglass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiberglass "Fiberglass") and [phenolic resin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenolic_resin "Phenolic resin"), was formerly used on the Apollo missions and on the [Space Shuttle orbiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Shuttle_orbiter "Space Shuttle orbiter") for early flights.[\[23\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-24)

The CM uses [glass cockpit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glass_cockpit "Glass cockpit") digital control systems derived from those of the [Boeing 787](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boeing_787_Dreamliner "Boeing 787 Dreamliner").[\[24\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-fi_20061006_orion-25)
It incorporates an "autodock" feature, like those of [Progress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Progress_spacecraft "Progress spacecraft"), the [Automated Transfer Vehicle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automated_Transfer_Vehicle "Automated Transfer Vehicle"), and [Dragon 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX_Dragon_2 "SpaceX Dragon 2"), with provision for the flight crew to take over in an emergency.
It has waste-management facilities, with a miniature camping-style toilet and the unisex "relief tube" used on the Space Shuttle.
It has a nitrogen/oxygen (N

2/O

2) mixed [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere "Atmosphere") at either sea level (101.3 [kPa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit) "Pascal (unit)") or 14.69 [psi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound_per_square_inch "Pound per square inch")) or reduced (55.2 to 70.3kPa or 8.01 to 10.20psi) pressure.

The CM is built of [aluminium-lithium alloy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium-lithium_alloy "Aluminium-lithium alloy"). The reusable recovery parachutes are based on the parachutes used on both the [Apollo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_program "Apollo program") spacecraft and the [Space Shuttle Solid Rocket Boosters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Solid_Rocket_Booster "Space Shuttle Solid Rocket Booster"), and constructed of [Nomex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nomex "Nomex") cloth. Water landing is the exclusive means of recovery for the Orion CM.[\[25\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-NSF-20070805-26)[\[26\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-27)

To allow Orion to mate with other vehicles, it will be equipped with the [NASA Docking System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA_Docking_System "NASA Docking System"). The spacecraft employs a [Launch Abort System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Launch_abort_system "Launch abort system") (LAS) along with a "Boost Protective Cover" (made of [fiberglass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiberglass "Fiberglass")), to protect the Orion CM from [aerodynamic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerodynamics "Aerodynamics") and impact stresses during the first 2+1⁄2 minutes of ascent. Orion is designed to be 10 times safer during ascent and reentry than the [Space Shuttle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Shuttle "Space Shuttle").[\[27\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-decision-28) The CM is designed to be refurbished and reused. In addition, all of Orion's component parts have been designed to be as modular as possible, so that between the craft's first test flight in 2014 and its projected Mars voyage in the 2030s, the spacecraft can be upgraded as new technologies become available.[\[15\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-Space.com1-16)

As of 2019, the [Spacecraft Atmospheric Monitor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacecraft_Atmospheric_Monitor "Spacecraft Atmospheric Monitor") is planned to be used in the Orion CM.[\[28\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-29)

### European Service Module (ESM)

Main article: [European Service Module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Service_Module "European Service Module")

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Orion_Service_Module.jpg/500px-Orion_Service_Module.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Orion_Service_Module.jpg) An artist's concept of an Orion spacecraft including the European Service Module with [Interim Cryogenic Upper Stage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interim_Cryogenic_Upper_Stage "Interim Cryogenic Upper Stage") attached at the back

In May 2011, the [ESA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ESA "ESA") director general announced a possible collaboration with [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA") to work on a successor to the [Automated Transfer Vehicle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automated_Transfer_Vehicle "Automated Transfer Vehicle") (ATV).[\[29\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-30) On June 21, 2012, two studies by Astrium were made public. The first looked into the possible construction of a service module which would be used in tandem with the Orion CM.[\[30\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-SFN-20120621-31) The second examined the possible production of a versatile multi purpose orbital vehicle.[\[31\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-32)

On November 21, 2012, the ESA decided to develop an [ATV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automated_Transfer_Vehicle "Automated Transfer Vehicle")-derived service module for Orion.[\[32\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-NSF-20121121-33) The service module is being manufactured by Airbus Defence and Space in [Bremen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bremen "Bremen"), Germany.[\[33\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-AirbusBremen-34) [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA") announced on January 16, 2013, that the ESA service module will first fly on [Artemis I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_I "Artemis I"), the debut launch of the Space Launch System.[\[34\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-osm-35)

Testing of the European service module began in February 2016, at the [Space Power Facility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Power_Facility "Space Power Facility").[\[35\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-NSF-20151201-36)

On February 16, 2017, a €200 million contract was signed between [Airbus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airbus "Airbus") and the European Space Agency for the production of a second [European service module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_service_module "Orion service module") for use on the first crewed Orion flight, [Artemis II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_II "Artemis II").[\[36\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-37)

On October 26, 2018, the first unit for Artemis I was assembled in full at Airbus Defence and Space's factory in Bremen, Germany.[\[37\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-ESABremen2018-38)

### Launch Abort System (LAS)

See also: [Orion abort modes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_abort_modes "Orion abort modes")

In the event of an emergency on the [launch pad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Launch_pad "Launch pad") or during ascent, the [Launch Abort System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Launch_abort_system "Launch abort system") (LAS) will separate the crew module from the launch vehicle using three [solid rocket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_rocket "Solid rocket") motors: an abort motor (AM),[\[38\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-39) an attitude control motor (ACM), and a jettison motor (JM). The AM provides the thrust needed to accelerate the capsule, while the ACM is used to point the AM[\[39\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-40) and the jettison motor separates the LAS from the crew capsule.[\[40\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-41) On July 10, 2007, [Orbital Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_Sciences "Orbital Sciences"), the prime contractor for the LAS, awarded [Alliant Techsystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alliant_Techsystems "Alliant Techsystems") (ATK) a $62.5 million sub-contract to "design, develop, produce, test and deliver the launch abort motor," which uses a "reverse flow" design.[\[41\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-42) On July 9, 2008, [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA") announced that ATK had completed construction of a vertical test stand at a facility in [Promontory, Utah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Promontory,_Utah "Promontory, Utah") to test launch abort motors for the Orion spacecraft.[\[42\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-43) Another long-time space motor contractor, [Aerojet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerojet "Aerojet"), was awarded the jettison motor design and development contract for the LAS. As of September 2008, [Aerojet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerojet "Aerojet") has, along with team members Orbital Sciences, [Lockheed Martin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lockheed_Martin "Lockheed Martin") and NASA, successfully demonstrated two full-scale test firings of the jettison motor. This motor is used on every flight, as it separates the LAS from the vehicle after both a successful launch and a launch abort.[\[43\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-44)

### Spacecraft properties and performance

With the announcement in 2019 of the intent to procure a [Human Landing System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Landing_System "Human Landing System") for Artemis missions, NASA provided Orion mass and propulsion capability values. After separation from the SLS upper stage, the Orion is expected to have a mass of 26,375kg (58,147lb) and be capable of performing maneuvers requiring up to 1,050m/s (3,445ft/s) of [delta-v](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delta-v "Delta-v").[\[44\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-45)

## History

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/NASA%27s_Orion_Spacecraft_Heads_Cross_Country.jpeg/500px-NASA%27s_Orion_Spacecraft_Heads_Cross_Country.jpeg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:NASA's_Orion_Spacecraft_Heads_Cross_Country.jpeg) Transport of the Orion capsule before the first test (2013)

### Potential Orion program cancellation\
\
On 2 May 2025, the Trump administration released its fiscal year 2026 budget proposal for NASA, which calls for terminating the Orion and SLS programs after Artemis III.[\[89\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-arstech5-90)[\[12\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-:3-13) The budget allocates funding for a program to transition to "more cost-effective commercial systems".[\[90\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-91) This proposal was rejected by Congress on July 2025 however, favoring the continuation of the program alongside the SLS and the Lunar Gateway.[\[91\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-arstech1-92)\

- [Space Vehicle Mockup Facility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Vehicle_Mockup_Facility "Space Vehicle Mockup Facility") (SVMF) in [Johnson Space Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johnson_Space_Center "Johnson Space Center"), includes a full-scale Orion capsule mock-up for astronaut training.[\[92\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-93)\
- [MLAS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Launch_Abort_System "Max Launch Abort System") An Orion boilerplate was used in the MLAS test launch.\

- [Ascent Abort-2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ascent_Abort-2 "Ascent Abort-2") An Orion boilerplate was used for the Ascent Abort 2 flight test, the LAS was fully functional, the boilerplate was discarded.\

This same test article has been modified to support Orion Recovery Testing in stationary and underway recovery tests.[\[93\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-94) The BTA contains over 150 sensors to gather data on its test drops.[\[94\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-95) Testing of the 18,000-pound (8,200kg) mockup ran from July 2011 to January 6, 2012.[\[95\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-96)\
- The Ground Test Article (GTA) stack, at Lockheed Martin in Denver, Colorado, was undergoing vibration testing.[\[96\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-NSF-20111106-97) It is made up of the Orion Ground Test Vehicle (GTV) combined with its Launch Abort System (LAS). Drogue chutes deploy around 20,000 and 15,000 feet (6,100 and 4,600m). Testing of the [staged](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reefing "Reefing") parachutes includes the partial opening and complete failure of one of the three main parachutes. With only two chutes deployed, the DTA lands at 33 feet per second (10m/s), the maximum touchdown speed for Orion's design.[\[98\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-99) The drop test program had several failures in 2007, 2008, and 2010,[\[99\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-NSF-20120210-100) resulting in a new DTV being constructed. The landing parachute set is known as the Capsule Parachute Assembly System (CPAS).[\[100\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-NSF-20120226-101) With all parachutes functional, a landing speed of 17mph (27km/h) was achieved.[\[101\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-102) A third test vehicle, the PCDTV3, was successfully tested in a drop on April 17, 2012.[\[102\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-NSF-20120412-103)\

## Variants\

### Orion Crew Exploration Vehicle (CEV)\
\
Main article: [Crew Exploration Vehicle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crew_Exploration_Vehicle "Crew Exploration Vehicle")\
\
[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Orion_spacecraft_launch_configuration_%282009_revision%29.jpg/500px-Orion_spacecraft_launch_configuration_%282009_revision%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Orion_spacecraft_launch_configuration_(2009_revision).jpg) Orion CEV design as of 2009\
\
The idea for a Crew Exploration Vehicle (CEV) was announced on January 14, 2004, as part of the [Vision for Space Exploration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vision_for_Space_Exploration "Vision for Space Exploration") after the [Space Shuttle _Columbia_ accident](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Columbia_disaster "Space Shuttle Columbia disaster").[\[103\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-104) The CEV effectively replaced the conceptual [Orbital Space Plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_Space_Plane "Orbital Space Plane") (OSP), a proposed replacement for the Space Shuttle. A design competition was held, and the winner was the proposal from a consortium led by Lockheed Martin. It was subsequently named "Orion" in 2006[\[104\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-ann20260823-105) after the stellar [constellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(constellation) "Orion (constellation)") and [mythical hunter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(mythology) "Orion (mythology)") of the same name,[\[105\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-106) and became part of the [Constellation program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constellation_program "Constellation program") under NASA administrator [Sean O'Keefe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sean_O'Keefe "Sean O'Keefe"). As of 2006[\[update\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Orion_(spacecraft)&action=edit), NASA planned for the first flight to the International Space Station with astronauts onboard to be no later than 2014.[\[104\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-ann20260823-105)\
\
Constellation proposed using the Orion CEV in both crew and cargo variants to support the [International Space Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Space_Station "International Space Station") and as a crew vehicle for a return to the Moon. The crew/command module was originally intended to land on solid ground on the US west coast using airbags but later changed to ocean splashdown, while a service module was included for life support and propulsion.[\[25\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-NSF-20070805-26) With a diameter of 5 meters (16ft 5in) as opposed to 3.9 meters (12ft 10in), the Orion CEV would have provided 2.5 times greater volume than the Apollo CM.[\[106\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-107) The service module was originally planned to use liquid [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#Liquid_methane_rocket_fuel "Methane") (CH4) as its fuel, but switched to [hypergolic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypergolic "Hypergolic") propellants due to the infancy of oxygen/methane-powered rocket technologies and the goal of launching the first uncrewed Orion CEV by 2012.[\[107\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-108)[\[108\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-109)[\[109\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-110)\
\
The Orion CEV was to be launched on the [Ares I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ares_I "Ares I") rocket to low Earth orbit, where it would rendezvous with the [Altair lunar lander](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altair_(spacecraft) "Altair (spacecraft)") launched on a heavy-lift [Ares V](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ares_V "Ares V") launch vehicle for lunar missions.\
\
#### Environmental testing\
\
NASA performed environmental testing of Orion from 2007 to 2011 at the [Glenn Research Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glenn_Research_Center "Glenn Research Center") Plum Brook Station in [Sandusky, Ohio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sandusky,_Ohio "Sandusky, Ohio"). The Center's [Space Power Facility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Power_Facility "Space Power Facility") is the world's largest [thermal vacuum chamber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_vacuum_chamber "Thermal vacuum chamber").[\[110\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-111)\
\
#### Launch Abort System (LAS) testing\
\
[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Orion_CM-LAS_stack.jpg/500px-Orion_CM-LAS_stack.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Orion_CM-LAS_stack.jpg) Orion [LAS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Launch_abort_system "Launch abort system") test assembled at the NASA Research Center\
\
ATK Aerospace successfully completed the first Orion Launch Abort System (LAS) test on November 20, 2008. The LAS motor could provide 500,000 [lbf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound_(force) "Pound (force)") (2,200 [kN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_(unit) "Newton (unit)")) of thrust in case an emergency situation should arise on the launch pad or during the first 300,000 feet (91km) of the rocket's climb to orbit.[\[111\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-112)\
\
On March 2, 2009, a full size, full weight command module mockup (pathfinder) began its journey from the Langley Research Center to [White Sands Missile Range](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_Sands_Missile_Range "White Sands Missile Range") in southern [New Mexico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Mexico "New Mexico") for at-gantry launch vehicle assembly training and for LES testing.[\[112\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-113) On May 10, 2010, NASA successfully executed the LES PAD-Abort-1 test at White Sands, launching a boilerplate (mock-up) Orion capsule to an altitude of approximately 6,000 feet (1,800m). The test used three solid-fuel rocket motors–the main thrust motor, an attitude control motor and the jettison motor.[\[113\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-114)\
\
#### Splashdown recovery testing\
\
In 2009, during the Constellation phase of the program, the Post-landing Orion Recovery Test (PORT) was designed to determine and evaluate methods of crew rescue and what kind of motions the astronaut crew could expect after landing, including conditions outside the capsule for the recovery team. The evaluation process supported NASA's design of landing recovery operations including equipment, ship and crew needs.\
\
The PORT Test used a full-scale [boilerplate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boilerplate_(spaceflight) "Boilerplate (spaceflight)") (mock-up) of NASA's Orion crew module and was tested in water under simulated and real weather conditions. Tests began March 23, 2009, with a Navy-built, 18,000-pound (8,200kg) boilerplate in a test pool. Full sea testing ran April 6–30, 2009, at various locations off the coast of NASA's Kennedy Space Center with media coverage.[\[114\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-115)\
\
#### Cancellation of Constellation program\
\
[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Orion_lunar_orbit_%28Sept_2006%29.jpg/500px-Orion_lunar_orbit_%28Sept_2006%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Orion_lunar_orbit_(Sept_2006).jpg) Artist's conception of Orion (as then-designed) in lunar orbit\
\
On May 7, 2009, the Obama administration enlisted the [Augustine Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Review_of_United_States_Human_Space_Flight_Plans_Committee "Review of United States Human Space Flight Plans Committee") to perform a full independent review of the ongoing NASA space exploration program. The commission found the then-current [Constellation Program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constellation_Program "Constellation Program") to be woefully under-budgeted with significant cost overruns, behind schedule by four years or more in several essential components, and unlikely to be capable of meeting any of its scheduled goals.[\[115\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-116)[\[116\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-117) As a consequence, the commission recommended a significant re-allocation of goals and resources. As one of the many outcomes based on these recommendations, on October 11, 2010, the Constellation program was canceled, ending development of the Altair, Ares I, and Ares V. The Orion Crew Exploration Vehicle survived the cancellation and was transferred to be launched on the Space Launch System.[\[117\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-118)\
\
### Orion Multi-Purpose Crew Vehicle (MPCV)\
\
The Orion development program was restructured from three different versions of the Orion capsule, each for a different task,[\[118\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-119) to the development of the MPCV as a single version capable of performing multiple tasks.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-OrionQuickFacts-3) On December 5, 2014, a developmental Orion spacecraft was successfully launched into space and retrieved at sea after splashdown on the [Exploration Flight Test-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exploration_Flight_Test-1 "Exploration Flight Test-1") (EFT-1).[\[119\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-120)[\[120\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-121)\
\
#### Orion splashdown recovery testing\
\
[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Delta_IV_Heavy_on_pad_with_Orion_EFT-1_%28KSC-2014-4686%29.jpg/500px-Delta_IV_Heavy_on_pad_with_Orion_EFT-1_%28KSC-2014-4686%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Delta_IV_Heavy_on_pad_with_Orion_EFT-1_(KSC-2014-4686).jpg) [EFT-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exploration_Flight_Test-1 "Exploration Flight Test-1") on top of a [Delta IV Heavy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delta_IV_Heavy "Delta IV Heavy")\
\
Before EFT-1 in December 2014, several preparatory vehicle recovery tests were performed, which continued the "crawl, walk, run" approach established by PORT. The "crawl" phase was performed August 12–16, 2013, with the Stationary Recovery Test (SRT).\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\] The SRT demonstrated the recovery hardware and techniques that were to be employed for the recovery of the Orion CM in the protected waters of [Naval Station Norfolk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naval_Station_Norfolk "Naval Station Norfolk") using the LPD-17 type [USS Arlington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/USS_Arlington_(LPD-24) "USS Arlington (LPD-24)") as the recovery ship.[\[121\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-122)\
\
The "walk" and "run" phases were performed with the Underway Recovery Test (URT). Also using an LPD 17 class ship, the URT was performed in more realistic sea conditions off the coast of California in early 2014 to prepare the US Navy / NASA team for recovering the Exploration Flight Test-1 (EFT-1) Orion CM. The URT tests completed the pre-launch test phase of the Orion recovery system.\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]\

### Orion Lite\
\
#### History\
\
**Orion Lite** is an unofficial name used in the media for a lightweight crew capsule proposed by [Bigelow Aerospace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bigelow_Aerospace "Bigelow Aerospace") in collaboration with Lockheed Martin. It was to be based on the Orion spacecraft that Lockheed Martin was developing for NASA. It was never developed. It was to be a lighter, less capable and a less expensive version of the full Orion.[\[122\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-Space.com3-123)\
\
Orion Lite was intended to provide a stripped-down version of the Orion that would be available for missions to the International Space Station earlier than the more capable Orion, which is designed for longer duration missions to the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") and [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars").[\[123\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-MSNBCspace200908-124)\
\
Bigelow had begun working with Lockheed Martin in 2004. A few years later Bigelow signed a million-dollar contract to develop "an Orion mockup, an Orion Lite",[\[124\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-tsr20101101-125) in 2009.[\[122\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-Space.com3-123)\
\
The proposed collaboration between Bigelow and Lockheed Martin on the Orion Lite spacecraft has ended.\[ _[when?](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items "Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers")_\] Bigelow began work with [Boeing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boeing "Boeing") on a similar capsule, the [CST-100](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CST-100 "CST-100"), which has no Orion heritage, and was one of the two systems selected under NASA's [Commercial Crew Development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commercial_Crew_Development "Commercial Crew Development") (CCDev) program to transport crew to the ISS.[\[125\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-126)\
\
#### Design\
\
Orion Lite's primary mission would be to transport crew to the International Space Station (ISS), or to private space stations such as the planned [B330](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B330 "B330") from Bigelow Aerospace. While Orion Lite would have the same exterior dimensions as the Orion, there would be no need for the deep space infrastructure present in the Orion configuration. As such, the Orion Lite would have been able to support larger crews of around 7 people as the result of greater habitable interior volume and the reduced weight of equipment needed to support an exclusively low-Earth-orbit configuration.[\[126\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-PS20090814-127)\
\
#### Recovery\
\
In order to reduce the weight of Orion Lite, the more durable heat shield of the Orion would be replaced with a lighter weight heat shield designed to support the lower temperatures of Earth atmospheric re-entry from low Earth orbit. Additionally, the current proposal calls for a [mid-air retrieval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mid-air_retrieval "Mid-air retrieval"), wherein another aircraft captures the descending Orion Lite module.\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\] To date, such a retrieval method has not been employed for crewed spacecraft, although it [has been used with satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discoverer_14 "Discoverer 14").[\[127\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-128)\

#### Potential Mars missions\
\
[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Orion_docked_to_Mars_Transfer_Vehicle.jpg/960px-Orion_docked_to_Mars_Transfer_Vehicle.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Orion_docked_to_Mars_Transfer_Vehicle.jpg) Artist rendering of the Orion CEV docked to a proposed Mars Transfer Vehicle\
\
Further information: [Human mission to Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_mission_to_Mars "Human mission to Mars")\
\
The Orion capsule is designed to support future missions to send astronauts to Mars, probably to take place in the 2030s. Since the Orion capsule provides only about 2.25m3 (79cuft) of living space per crew member,[\[132\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-Repo-133) the use of an additional [Deep Space Habitat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_Space_Habitat "Deep Space Habitat") (DSH) module featuring propulsion will be needed for long-duration missions. The complete spacecraft stack is known as the [Deep Space Transport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_Space_Transport "Deep Space Transport").[\[133\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-Inverse_2017-134) The habitat module will provide additional space and supplies, as well as facilitate spacecraft maintenance, mission communications, exercise, training, and personal recreation.[\[134\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-early_dsh_proposal-135) Some concepts for DSH modules would provide approximately 70.0m3 (2,472cuft) of living space per crew member,[\[134\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-early_dsh_proposal-135) though the DSH module is in its early conceptual stage. DSH sizes and configurations may vary slightly, depending on crew and mission needs.[\[135\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-136) The mission may launch in the mid-2030s or late-2030s.[\[136\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-SN-20190418-137)\
\

#### Asteroid Redirect Mission\
\
Main article: [Asteroid Redirect Mission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_Redirect_Mission "Asteroid Redirect Mission")\
\
The **Asteroid Redirect Mission** ( **ARM**), also known as the **Asteroid Retrieval and Utilization** ( **ARU**) mission and the **Asteroid Initiative**, was a [space mission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_mission "Space mission") proposed by [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA") in 2013. The Asteroid Retrieval Robotic Mission (ARRM) spacecraft would rendezvous with a large [near-Earth asteroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near-Earth_asteroid "Near-Earth asteroid") and use robotic arms with anchoring grippers to retrieve a 4-meter boulder from the asteroid. A secondary objective was to develop the required technology to bring a small [near-Earth asteroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near-Earth_asteroid "Near-Earth asteroid") into [lunar orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_orbit "Lunar orbit") – "the asteroid was a bonus." There, it could be analyzed by the crew of the [Orion EM-5 or EM-6](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#Flights) ARCM mission in 2026.[\[137\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)#cite_note-SN-20170614-138)\
